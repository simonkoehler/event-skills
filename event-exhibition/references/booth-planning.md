## Booth Types and Sizing

| Type | Description | Typical Size | Open Sides | Visibility | Price (ratio to Inline) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Inline / Linear | Against a wall, neighbors on both sides | 6-12 m² | 1 side | Lowest | 1.0× (baseline) |
| Corner | End of a row, two open sides | 9-16 m² | 2 sides | Medium | 1.2-1.5× |
| Peninsula | Three open sides, one back wall | 16-36 m² | 3 sides | High | 1.5-2.0× |
| Island | Open on all sides, standalone | 25-100+ m² | 4 sides | Highest | 2.0-3.0× |

**Right-sizing your booth:**
- First-time exhibitor: Start with 9-12 m². You can always upgrade next year.
- Staff rule: 1 person per 3-4 m² of booth space. A 12 m² booth needs 3-4 staff.
- Meeting area eats space. A table + 4 chairs needs 4-6 m². Budget for it or skip it.
- Demo station: 2-3 m² per station including standing room for viewers.
- Storage: at least 1 m² for bags, boxes, backup materials. It fills up fast.

## Logistics Checklist

### T-12 to T-8 weeks
- [ ] Booth space booked and confirmed with organizer
- [ ] Booth builder contracted (or DIY plan finalized)
- [ ] Design brief sent to booth builder
- [ ] Shipping logistics planned (if equipment travels)
- [ ] Hotel rooms booked for team (near venue — book early, conference hotels sell out)

### T-8 to T-4 weeks
- [ ] Design approved, production started
- [ ] Electrical and internet ordered from organizer (they have deadlines — miss them and pay double)
- [ ] Furniture ordered (if not included)
- [ ] Giveaways / collateral ordered (allow 3-4 weeks for production)
- [ ] Lead scanning app configured and tested
- [ ] Pre-show outreach campaign started (see Output E)
- [ ] Stand personnel identified, shifts planned

### T-4 to T-1 week
- [ ] Graphics printed and shipped or confirmed
- [ ] Equipment shipped to venue (tracking number confirmed)
- [ ] Stand personnel briefing scheduled
- [ ] Meeting schedule with pre-booked target accounts confirmed
- [ ] Backup plans documented (what if shipment is late? what if demo breaks?)

### On-site
- [ ] Arrive for build-up. Verify everything arrived.
- [ ] Set up and test all equipment and demos
- [ ] Brief all staff on-site (even if pre-briefed — context changes)
- [ ] Test lead scanning with a real badge scan
- [ ] Take photos of the finished booth (before visitors — for future reference)
- [ ] Daily debrief: review leads, adjust approach, share learnings

### Post-show
- [ ] Pack up within teardown window (venues charge penalties for overtime)
- [ ] Confirm all equipment shipped back
- [ ] Upload all leads to CRM within 24 hours
- [ ] Follow-up A-leads within 24h, B-leads within 48h
- [ ] Post-show report within 1 week

## Giveaway Strategy (two-tier model)

Treat giveaways as two distinct categories with different purposes, costs, and qualification gates. Mixing them creates waste and undermines lead quality.

### Tier 1: Brand-recall items (cheap, mass-distributed)

**Purpose:** Stay top-of-mind after the show. The recipient doesn't need to be a qualified lead — they just need to use the item later.

**Examples:** Branded pens, stickers, simple water bottles, lanyards (if event allows custom), tote bags, mints/snacks.

**Cost guidance:** ≤2% of per-attendee booth budget. Quantity = expected booth visitors × 1.2 (some take more than one).

**Distribution rule:** Available openly on the booth counter. No qualification gate. Anyone walking by can take one.

### Tier 2: Lead-quality items (premium, qualification-gated)

**Purpose:** Reward genuine engagement and capture A/B-tier leads. The item itself is part of the conversation.

**Examples:** Wireless chargers, branded notebooks, conference-branded hoodies, hardcover books (especially books written by your CEO/team), high-end pens, AirTags, premium socks.

**Cost guidance:** 5-15× the cost of a Tier-1 item. Quantity = expected A+B lead count × 1.1, not booth-visitor count.

**Distribution rule:** Only after badge scan + a real conversation. Phrase: "We have a small thank-you for folks who're seriously evaluating [topic] — let me grab one." Implicit qualification gate.

### Anti-patterns to avoid

- **Hoodies on the counter:** burns ~80% of budget on disqualified leads. Premium items must be gated.
- **Bowls of branded pens with no human contact:** pens get used as ammunition by kids, not by your buyers. If staff can't see who takes the pen, save the money.
- **"Scan to win a [iPad / drone / TV]":** generates volume but ~70% disqualified leads (raffle hunters). Use only if your team can absorb the noise — most can't.
- **Same giveaway tier-1 and tier-2:** undermines the qualification gate. Different visual signal required.

### Sustainability note

Decline branded swag entirely if budget is tight or sustainability is a stated value. Replace with digital alternatives: a curated resource page (PDF + 3-5 links), a follow-up book recommendation, or a personalized 30-min consult slot. Digital deliverables often convert better than physical items for the A/B leads anyway.

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Happens | How to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Too much furniture | "We need a meeting table AND a demo area AND a lounge AND..." | Choose ONE primary function. Everything else is secondary. |
| Closed-off booth | Table across the front creates a barrier | Open front. Staff stands in front of the table, not behind it. |
| No meeting area | "We don't need one — we're doing demos" | Even 2 chairs in a quiet corner saves deals. Top-level conversations don't happen standing in an aisle. |
| Boring giveaways | "Everyone gives out pens, so we should too" | Give something useful AND memorable. Or give nothing — save the budget. |
| No lead follow-up plan | "We'll figure it out when we get back" | Plan follow-up BEFORE the show. Draft emails before you travel. |
| Shipping too late | "It'll arrive in time" | Ship 5 business days early. Venues have strict receiving windows. |
| Electrical ordered last minute | "It's just a plug, right?" | Organizers charge 2-3x for late electrical orders. Order with booth space. |
| Team doesn't know the pitch | "They know our product" | Knowing the product ≠ knowing the 60-second booth pitch. Practice. |
