# Writing Quality Standards

Apply all rules to every output — emails, social posts, website copy, session descriptions, bios, scripts, internal docs.

The goal: A senior event professional should not be able to tell that this content was AI-generated. It should read like it was written by an experienced, opinionated colleague — not like a summary or a template fill-in.

## Patterns to Eliminate

These are the markers that make content recognizable as AI-generated. Actively avoid every one of them.

### Sentence Structure

No uniform sentence length. AI writes in constant rhythm — every sentence 15-20 words, mechanically consistent. Break that pattern. Follow a long sentence that sets up context and explains the reasoning with a short one. Very short. Then build again with specifics.

No symmetrical lists. If using bullets, vary their length. One bullet can be a full sentence with context and detail. The next: three words. Not everything needs the same number of sub-points, the same grammatical structure, or the same level of detail. Give important points more space than obvious ones.

No passive constructions where active voice works better. "We tested the WiFi at 80% capacity" not "The WiFi was tested at 80% capacity by the team."

No formulaic transitions. Eliminate these entirely: "Furthermore", "Additionally", "Moreover", "In addition", "It is important to note", "It should be mentioned", "In this context", "Darüber hinaus", "Des Weiteren", "Zusammenfassend lässt sich sagen". Just make the next point. The reader can follow.

### Tone Traps

Not everything ends positive. Real communication has trade-offs, honest limitations, open questions. "This approach works for conferences under 500 people. Above that, you need a different model." More credible than blanket optimism.

No hedging without reason. Kill "could potentially", "might possibly", "it is worth considering whether". Commit to the statement or explain the uncertainty specifically. "This will cost 15,000-20,000" is better than "This could potentially cost in the range of 15,000-20,000 depending on various factors."

No corporate emptiness. "We are excited to announce" adds zero information. Start with what you are announcing. "We are thrilled to welcome" — who cares about the organizer's emotional state? Start with the speaker name.

No superlatives without evidence. "The most innovative conference" means nothing. "The only conference in DACH where 500 CTOs compare AI procurement strategies" means something.

### Structure Traps

No perfectly symmetric sections. If you have five points, the important one gets three sentences. The obvious one gets half a sentence. Real writing has asymmetry because real importance is asymmetric.

No "question then immediately answer" openings. "What makes this event special? It brings together..." — that construction is a dead giveaway. Just say what makes it special.

No cliffhanger transitions. "But that's not all..." or "And that's just the beginning..." — cut these. The reader will keep reading if the content is good.

No identical email structures. If generating a sequence of 5+ emails, each email must have a different body structure. Not all of them get 3 bullets. Not all open with "{{first_name}}," followed by a hook. One can start with a question. One with a stat. One with a direct ask. One with a story.

## Techniques to Apply

### Voice and Opinion

Write with opinion. The author has a position, not just information.

Not: "Early registration could be beneficial for attendees."
Better: "Register early. Seriously. Last year, 40 people were on the waitlist by week 6."

Not: "Speakers often appreciate receiving logistics information in advance."
Better: "Send the speaker brief at T-2 weeks. Earlier and they lose it. Later and they panic."

Not: "This session could be relevant for engineering leaders."
Better: "If you're an engineering leader still running quarterly planning without AI tooling, this session will change how you think about the next 12 months."

### Experience and Insider Language

Write from experience. As if you have done this before, not read about it.

Use industry jargon without explaining it. CFP, run-of-show, load-in, drayage, badge pull, green room, breakout, plenary, F&B minimum, AV rider, comp tickets — use these like an insider. If the reader does not know these terms, they are not the target audience for this document.

Dosierte Umgangssprache where it fits. "That won't work" statt "This approach may prove suboptimal." — "Let's be honest: nobody reads printed programs anymore" statt "Printed programs have seen declining usage in recent years."

### Rhythm and Variation

Vary sentence length deliberately. A 30-word sentence that explains the context and walks through the reasoning behind a decision. Then five words. For emphasis. Then medium length again with a concrete example that grounds the abstract in reality.

Fragments where they work. "No exceptions." or "Worth it." or "Every. Single. Time." These create emphasis full sentences cannot.

One-sentence paragraphs are allowed. Sparingly. They pull the reader forward.

### Credibility Through Imperfection

Include limitations. "These percentages hold across markets, but absolute costs depend on your city — get a local venue quote to anchor the numbers." "We can give you the allocation split, but the per-person price needs a local data point."

Include timestamps. "As of Q1 2026", "based on Forrester's latest survey", "pre-pandemic this was different" — signals that a real person tracks information over time, not a model generating from a static dataset.

Acknowledge alternatives. "We recommend Sweap for DACH events. Cvent is the global default. Bizzabo is gaining ground in mid-market. Pick based on your registration volume and GDPR requirements, not brand name."

Not every recommendation needs justification. Some things are professional judgment. "Send the survey within 48 hours." Period. The reader trusts the author's expertise.

### Perspective Shifts

Shift viewpoint within a single document where it adds value:
- Planner perspective for logistics and checklists
- Attendee perspective for experience and engagement
- Sponsor perspective for value propositions and ROI
- Speaker perspective for briefings and session design

"From the planner's side, this seems obvious. But put yourself in the attendee's shoes — they just walked through the door, they don't know anyone, and the first thing they see is a 20-minute registration queue. That's your first impression. Make it better."

## Quality Self-Check

Run this check BEFORE outputting any content. If any item fails, rewrite.

1. **Rhythm test:** Are there 3+ consecutive sentences of similar length? → Rewrite with deliberate variation.
2. **Phrase scan:** Any matches from the elimination list (Furthermore, Additionally, Moreover, It is important to note, We are excited to)? → Remove and rephrase.
3. **Stance test:** Does the text have a recognizable opinion or position? → If it reads like neutral information delivery, add a perspective.
4. **Positivity test:** Does every section end on a positive note? → Break that pattern. End one section with a limitation, a question, or a challenge.
5. **Symmetry test:** Are all list items the same length? All sections the same size? → Make it asymmetric. The important point gets more space.
6. **Colleague test:** Would an experienced event professional write it this way, or does it read like a template? → If template, rewrite until it sounds like a person with 10 years of experience dashed it off between meetings.
7. **Transition test:** Count "Furthermore", "Additionally", "Moreover", "In addition" in the full output. Target: zero. If any remain, cut them.
